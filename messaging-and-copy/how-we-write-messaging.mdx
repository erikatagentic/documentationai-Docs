---
title: "How We Write Your Outbound Messaging"
---

We don't use templates. Every sequence is written from scratch based on research into your business, your market, and your ideal customer. Here's how the process works.

## Step 1: Research

Before we write a single word, we dig into your business. We scrape your website (not just the homepage, but about pages, case studies, services, pricing), check Google Reviews, look at your LinkedIn presence, and research 2-3 of your competitors. The goal is to understand what makes you different in language your prospects would actually use, not marketing speak.

We also validate your ICP. If your case studies feature mid-market retail companies but you're asking us to target enterprise SaaS, we'll flag that disconnect before writing.

## Step 2: Writing

Each campaign gets a full outreach sequence:
- Connection request (often left blank, sometimes with a short note)
- 3 follow-up messages, spaced 1-7 days apart
- 1 InMail for reaching non-connections

We write 2 variants per message type (A and B) so we can test what resonates. Messages are short by design. Follow-ups are typically 30-50 words. Anything longer and response rates drop.

## Step 3: Your Review

We send you the messaging document before anything goes live. You review it, give feedback, and we adjust. One round of revisions is standard. See "How to Review Your Messaging" for tips on giving feedback that speeds this up.

## Step 4: A/B Testing

Once campaigns are running, we monitor which variant performs better. After enough data (usually 2-3 weeks), we can kill the underperformer and either scale the winner or test a new challenger against it.

## Why Our Messages Sound Different

You might notice our messaging doesn't sound like typical LinkedIn outreach. That's intentional. We avoid corporate buzzwords, exclamation marks, and generic openers like "I noticed you're in [industry]." Those patterns scream automation and get ignored.

Instead we focus on specificity. A real detail from your business, a concrete result, a question that shows we actually know what the prospect deals with. That's what gets replies.
